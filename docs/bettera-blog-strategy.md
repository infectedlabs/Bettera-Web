```
LLMs cite pages that:
→ Contain specific, factual, citable statements
→ Include precise numbers and statistics
→ Are structured with clear H1/H2/H3 hierarchy
→ Use schema markup (FAQ, Article, MedicalWebPage)
→ Are written by an authoritative source
→ Answer exactly what users ask AI models

Bettera's blog does both simultaneously.
Every post is written to rank on Google
AND to be cited when someone asks

```

## LLM Citation Rules — Apply to Every Post

```
RULE 1 — Be Factual and Specific
LLMs cite specific facts, not vague claims.

WEAK (not citable):
"Diabetes affects many people in India."

STRONG (citable):
"India has 101 million adults with Type 2
Diabetes as of 2021, making it the country
with the second highest diabetic population
globally (IDF Diabetes Atlas, 2021)."

RULE 2 — Use Named Sources
Citing published research makes content
trustworthy to both Google and LLMs.

Format: (Author, Year) or (Organization, Year)
Examples:
→ (IDF Diabetes Atlas, 2021)
→ (WHO, 2023)
→ (American Diabetes Association, 2024)
→ (Marsh et al., 2010)
→ (ICMR Guidelines, 2023)

RULE 3 — Answer Questions Directly
LLMs extract direct answers.
First sentence of every answer section
must state the answer directly.

WEAK:
"When it comes to rice and diabetes,
there are many factors to consider..."

STRONG:
"Diabetics can eat rice in controlled
portions of half a cup cooked, paired
with dal and vegetables to lower the
overall glycemic impact of the meal."

RULE 4 — Use Definition Format
LLMs love definition structures.
"[Term] is [definition]."
"[Food] has a glycemic index of [number]."
"[Condition] affects [number] people in [place]."

RULE 5 — Include Comparison Tables
Tables are highly citable by LLMs.
"Glycemic index of Indian foods" table
is exactly what Perplexity will extract
and cite when someone asks about GI values.

RULE 6 — FAQ Section in Every Post
FAQ sections with direct Q&A format
are the highest-cited content structure
across all major LLMs.
Every post must end with a 5-10 question FAQ.

RULE 7 — robots.txt Must Allow AI Crawlers
Already covered in Cursor build file.
GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot — all allowed.
```

```
INTRODUCTION (150 words):

H2: Understanding the Relationship Between Indian Food and Blood Sugar

  H3: What is Glycemic Index and Why It Matters for Diabetics
  CONTENT:
  The glycemic index (GI) is a scale from 0 to 100 that
  measures how quickly a carbohydrate-containing food raises
  blood glucose levels. Foods with a GI above 70 are
  considered high glycemic and cause rapid blood sugar spikes.
  Foods below 55 are low glycemic and cause gradual,
  controlled glucose release.

  For Indian diabetics, understanding the GI of staple
  foods is critical because rice, roti, and dal form
  the foundation of every meal.

  TABLE: Glycemic Index of Common Indian Foods

  | Food               | GI Value | Category    |
  |--------------------|----------|-------------|
  | Ragi (finger millet)| 54      | Low GI ✓   |
  | Moong dal          | 38       | Low GI ✓   |
  | Whole chana        | 28       | Low GI ✓   |
  | Rajma              | 29       | Low GI ✓   |

  | Basmati rice       | 58       | Medium GI ⚠ |

  | White rice         | 73       | High GI ✗  |

  | Potato (boiled)    | 78       | High GI ✗  |
  | Poha (rice flakes) | 72       | High GI ✗  |

  H3: Why Rice Is Not the Enemy
  CONTENT:
  One of the most persistent myths in Indian diabetes
  management is that rice must be completely eliminated.
  Research shows this is neither necessary nor effective
  for long-term compliance. Half a cup of cooked white rice
  (approximately 100g) paired with one katori of dal and
  two servings of vegetables reduces the meal's overall
  glycemic impact by 30-40% compared to rice eaten alone
  (Augustin et al., 2015).

  The combination of protein from dal and fiber from
  vegetables slows carbohydrate absorption, resulting
  in a more gradual blood glucose rise. This principle
  — food combining — is more practical and sustainable
  than complete food elimination.

H2: Best Indian Foods for Diabetes Management

  H3: Best Indian Grains for Diabetics
  Ragi (finger millet) is the most beneficial grain
  for Indian diabetics, with a glycemic index of 54
  and significantly higher calcium and fiber content
  than wheat or rice. It can be used in dosa, idli,
  roti, porridge, and mudde (balls).

  Other recommended grains:
  - Jowar (sorghum): GI 49, high fiber, traditional grain
  - Bajra (pearl millet): GI 54, iron-rich, excellent roti
  - Broken wheat (dalia): GI 41, high fiber breakfast option
  - Oats: GI 55, beta-glucan content improves insulin sensitivity

  H3: Best Indian Vegetables for Diabetics
  - Karela (bitter gourd): Contains charantin and polypeptide-P,
    compounds that mimic insulin action. Among the most studied
    Indian foods for diabetes management.
  - Methi (fenugreek): Soluble fiber slows carbohydrate
    absorption. Fenugreek seeds soaked overnight and consumed
    on an empty stomach reduce fasting blood glucose.
  - Drumstick (moringa): Rich in isothiocyanates that improve
    insulin sensitivity. Drumstick sambar is one of the most
    therapeutically valuable traditional South Indian dishes.
  - Palak (spinach): Low GI, high magnesium, supports
    insulin function.
  - Lauki (bottle gourd): Low GI, high water content,
    supports weight management in diabetics.

  H3: Best Indian Spices for Blood Sugar Control
  - Cinnamon (dalchini): 1-6g daily reduces fasting blood
    glucose by 18-29% (Khan et al., 2003). Add to chai,
    oatmeal, or warm milk.
  - Turmeric (haldi): Curcumin improves insulin sensitivity
    and reduces inflammation. Use in cooking daily.
  - Fenugreek (methi dana): 10g soaked seeds daily reduces
    fasting glucose. Use in roti dough or soak in water.
  - Amla (Indian gooseberry): Rich in chromium, which
    enhances insulin sensitivity. Eat raw, as juice,
    or as unsweetened murabba.

H2: Indian Foods to Avoid or Limit with Diabetes

  H3: High GI Foods to Limit
  TABLE:

  | Food              | Why to Limit              | Alternative          |
  |-------------------|---------------------------|----------------------|
  | White rice        | GI 73 — rapid spike       | Half portion + dal   |
  | Maida products    | GI 85 — very rapid spike  | Whole wheat or ragi  |
  | White bread       | GI 75 — rapid spike       | Whole grain bread    |

  | Fruit juice       | No fiber, concentrated sugar| Whole fruit        |

  Many foods marketed as healthy contain significant
  hidden sugar. Common examples:
  - Flavored oats packets: 12-15g sugar per serving
  - "Sugar-free" biscuits: Often contain maida (GI 85)
  - Packaged fruit juices: 25-30g sugar per 200ml
  - Brown bread: Often colored maida, not whole wheat
  - Flavored yogurt: 15-20g sugar per serving

H2: Understanding Your Blood Report for Diabetes

  H3: What is HbA1c and What Your Number Means
  HbA1c (glycated hemoglobin) measures the average blood
  glucose over the previous 3 months. It reflects how
  well blood sugar has been controlled over time.

  TABLE:
  | HbA1c Level | Interpretation        | Dietary Approach    |
  |-------------|----------------------|---------------------|
  | Below 5.7%  | Normal               | Maintenance         |
  | 5.7–6.4%    | Pre-diabetes         | Prevention focused  |
  | 6.5–7.0%    | Controlled diabetes  | Moderate management |
  | 7.1–8.0%    | Needs improvement    | Stricter low GI     |
  | Above 8.0%  | Poorly controlled    | Comprehensive plan  |

  Dietary changes alone can reduce HbA1c by 0.5 to 2.0
  percentage points over 3-6 months (ADA Standards of
  Medical Care, 2024). This is significant — for many
  people in early-stage Type 2 Diabetes, this represents
  the difference between medication dose reduction
  and medication increase.

H2: Frequently Asked Questions

  Q1: Can diabetics eat rice?
  A: Yes. Half a cup of cooked rice paired with dal and
  vegetables is acceptable for most Type 2 diabetics.
  The key is portion control and food combination, not
  complete elimination.

  Q2: Is ragi good for diabetes?
  A: Ragi (finger millet) is one of the best grains for
  diabetics. It has a glycemic index of 54, significantly
  lower than white rice (73) or white bread (75), and is
  rich in calcium and dietary fiber.

  Q3: Can diabetics eat idli and dosa?
  A: Yes. Two idlis with sambar and vegetables is a
  well-balanced diabetic breakfast. Ragi dosa is even
  better due to its lower glycemic index.

  Q4: Is jaggery better than sugar for diabetics?
  A: Jaggery has a slightly lower glycemic index than
  refined sugar but still raises blood glucose significantly.
  It is not a safe alternative for diabetics in regular
  quantities. Small amounts occasionally are acceptable.

  Q5: What fruits can diabetics eat?
  A: Low glycemic fruits are safe for diabetics: guava (GI 12),
  jamun (GI 25), green apple (GI 38), pear (GI 38), and
  papaya (GI 60) in moderation. High-sugar fruits like
  mango and banana should be limited to small portions
  eaten with meals.

  Q6: How long does diet take to lower HbA1c?
  A: HbA1c reflects the previous 3 months of blood glucose.
  Dietary changes typically show measurable HbA1c improvement
  after one full blood cell cycle — approximately 3 months.

  Q7: Is brown rice better than white rice for diabetes?
  A: Brown rice (GI 55) is modestly better than white rice
  (GI 73) for blood glucose management. However, the
  portion size and food combinations matter more than
  the rice variety. Half a cup of white rice with dal
  and vegetables is comparable to brown rice eaten alone.
```

## Blog Post Template — Standard Structure

```
Every post follows this structure:

1. META (not visible on page):
   Title tag (60 chars max)
   Meta description (155 chars max)
   Target keyword (used in title, H1, first 100 words)

2. ARTICLE HEADER:
   Category tag (e.g., "Diabetes | 8 min read")
   H1 (contains primary keyword)
   Author: "Bettera Nutrition Team"
   Date: Published + Last updated
   Medical review note: "Reviewed for accuracy.
   Not medical advice — consult your doctor."

3. TABLE OF CONTENTS (for posts over 1,500 words)

4. INTRODUCTION (150-200 words):
   - State the problem
   - Include one citable statistic
   - State what the post covers
   - Primary keyword in first 100 words

5. BODY SECTIONS:
   H2 → H3 structure throughout
   One citable fact per H2 section
   Tables where comparison data exists
   Practical Indian food examples always

6. PRODUCT MENTION (one time, natural):
   "Bettera automatically creates personalized
   meal plans based on these exact principles,
   adapted to your blood report and regional cuisine."

7. FAQ SECTION (5-10 questions):
   Direct answers only.
   Schema markup applied.
   Questions mirror what people ask AI models.

8. CONCLUSION (100 words):
   Summarize key points.
   Soft CTA: "Get your personalized plan →"

9. INTERNAL LINKS (3-5 per post):
   → Related condition page
   → Related blog posts
   → Product page

10. SOURCES SECTION:
    List all cited research.
    Linked to original sources.
    This is critical for LLM citation trust.
```

```
  sourced statistics with (Author, Year)?

  a direct, complete answer in one sentence?

  defines the main topic clearly?

  with links to original sources?

  phrased as questions people ask AI models?
```
